the difference

Two ways an AI can move work.

On the left, the loop you already know: every step of progress passes back through you — a prompt, then work, then a prompt again. On the right, the flywheel: the AI drives itself and keeps spinning, breaking out to you only when it hits a decision it shouldn't make alone.

how it works · one loop

Propose. Queue. Drain.

The whole system is a single loop with a shared to-do list at the center. You describe outcomes; it turns them into concrete tasks — each with its own "done when…" test — then works them one at a time, smallest and safest first.

01 — propose

You describe the outcome

Plain language: "add dark mode", "fix the checkout bug", "clean up the settings page". No tickets, no ceremony.

→
02 — queue

It breaks the work down

Each request becomes concrete tasks with an acceptance test attached — done when… — so nothing drifts, even if the AI's memory resets mid-run.

→
03 — drain

It works the list

One task at a time, verifying each by actually running it. Finished work leaves the queue; anything needing your judgment gets set aside, not skipped.

the queue · where a human touches it

Every task is in one of four states.

This is the entire interface — a labelled list. The labels are the point: they mark exactly which items the machine owns and which ones are waiting on you. Nothing hides.

📋 openmachine owns it

Queued work, waiting its turn. The AI picks these up and completes them without asking. You never have to touch an open task — that's the whole point.

✓ doneleft the queue · verified

Finished and checked. The task was completed and exercised to confirm it works. Under autopilot, the change is committed to a review branch so you can see exactly what happened.

❓ parkedpushed out → your decision

A judgment call the machine won't make for you. A design direction, a wording choice, a new dependency, anything ambiguous or hard to undo. It's set aside with the full set of options and a recommendation — ready for you to pick, not re-explain.

⏳ blockedpushed out → your hands

Something only a person can do. A playtest, a login, a credential, approving a payment. The machine can't do it, so it flags it and moves on to everything else instead of stalling.

the flywheel · why work leaves the queue

A task exits for exactly two reasons.

Think of the queue as a spinning wheel. Work goes in; the wheel throws each piece into one of two bins. That sorting — done vs. yours — is the mechanism that lets it run alone safely.

✓ It finished the job

Reversible, routine, verifiable

The machine did the work, ran it, confirmed it, and moved on. This is most of the list — and all of it happens without you. The more decomposable and reversible your work is, the more falls into this bin.

❓ It hit your judgment

Irreversible, tasteful, or ambiguous

Instead of guessing — or worse, stopping the whole run to ask — it parks the decision with its options laid out, and keeps draining everything else. Momentum never stops for one fork in the road.

This is the honest heart of the thing. The AI is not trusted to decide what only you should decide. It's trusted to recognise the difference and route accordingly — do the safe work, set aside the rest. That boundary is what makes "leave it running overnight" a responsible sentence instead of a reckless one.

autopilot · the overnight run

Turn it on, fill the queue, walk away.

Autopilot is one switch. While it's on, the AI is not allowed to stop and wait — it keeps draining. Every turn's finished work is committed to its own review branch (never to your main code, never pushed anywhere). Decisions pile up quietly in the parked tray. This is the compounding part: eight idle hours become eight working hours.

safe by construction

It can't ship on its own

Work lands on a throwaway branch, never your main line and never pushed live. Nothing irreversible happens while you sleep — that's a rule, not a hope.

self-checking

It verifies as it goes

Each task carries its own "done when…". The AI runs the thing to confirm, rather than declaring victory. Real tests get written where they earn their keep.

never stalls

One fork can't freeze the run

Hit a decision? Park it and keep going. The whole list doesn't wait on one question — a stuck item never blocks the other thirty-nine.

the payoff · your morning review

You wake up to decisions, not a blank prompt.

Turn autopilot off and it flips into review mode. It walks you through the parked pile one item at a time — each as a short multiple-choice with a recommended answer already marked. You're arrow-keying through calls, not re-reading the codebase. Then you look over the branch of finished work and merge what's good.

Recommendation-first, always

Every parked item comes with the options and the AI's honest pick — including "don't do this at all". You confirm or override in seconds. No item is a blank "what do you want?"

The full context travelled with it

A parked decision carries the same reasoning it would have shown you live — so a decision made at 7 AM is as informed as one made in the moment, not a stub you have to reconstruct.

Then: review the branch, merge the good

Finished work sits on a review branch with clean, curated commits. You read the diff, keep what's right, and ship it — or send a fix back into the queue for the next run.

the ROI · what actually changes

It doesn't make you faster. It moves your time.

The win isn't that the AI types quicker — it already does. It's that the hours it spends stop needing to be your hours. Your judgment is the scarce resource; this spends it only where it counts, and lets the clock do the rest.

brutally honest · read this part twice

Where this helps — and where it won't.

This is not autonomous software that replaces you, and anyone selling it that way is lying. It concentrates your judgment; it does not remove it. Here's the unflattering truth.

⚠ The real limits

  • GARBAGE IN

    A vague queue at midnight is a wasted branch at 7 AM. An AI that never stops will build the wrong thing efficiently. The quality of your morning is set by the quality of what you queued — this rewards clear thinking, it doesn't replace it.

  • "VERIFIED" ≠ CORRECT

    Self-checking is not proof. It runs the code and confirms it does what the task said — but if the task was subtly wrong, so is the "done". You still review. Real safety comes from tests, and tests only exist where you asked for them.

  • STALLS HAPPEN

    Parking is a feature with a cost. If ten tasks all depend on one design decision you didn't pre-answer, the AI parks the decision and everything behind it waits. A great overnight run needs the big forks decided before you sleep.

  • NOT A GUI

    The entire interface is a text list and a status line. If you love Cursor for the polished editor, know that this lives in the terminal. The trade is deliberate — less to look at, more that runs itself — but it is a trade.

  • WRONG FOR SOME WORK

    Big, ambiguous, one-shot judgment calls don't decompose. If the whole task is the decision, there's nothing safe to drain in the background. This shines on batches of reversible, well-scoped work — and adds little to a single hard call.

None of these are hidden. The parked pile, the review branch, the "done when…" tests, the refusal to touch your main code — they exist because an AI running alone overnight is exactly as trustworthy as its guardrails. That's the honest pitch: not "trust the machine", but "trust the fence around it".

the bottom line

If your work is a list, this runs the list overnight and hands you the decisions.
If your work is one hard call, you're still the one making it.

The ROI is real and specific: it converts idle hours into finished, reviewable work, and converts your attention from "keep it going" into "decide what matters". Fill the queue, flip autopilot on, and spend tomorrow morning editing — not typing.
